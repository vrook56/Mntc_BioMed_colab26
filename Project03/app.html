Blood Pressure Measurement

Master the technique of measuring blood pressure using a manual sphygmomanometer

Patient: Sarah Martinez

Age
32 years
Gender
Female
Reason

Symptoms

1

Prepare the Equipment

Gather a properly sized blood pressure cuff (covers 80% of upper arm), sphygmomanometer, and stethoscope. Ensure the patient has been seated quietly for at least 5 minutes with feet flat on the floor.

The patient should avoid caffeine, exercise, and smoking for 30 minutes before measurement.
2

Position the Cuff

Place the cuff on the patient's bare upper arm, 1 inch above the antecubital fossa (elbow crease). The cuff's artery marker should align with the brachial artery. Ensure it's snug but not too tight—you should be able to fit two fingers under it.

💪

Cuff positioned on upper arm

3

Locate Brachial Pulse

Using your fingers (not thumb), locate the brachial artery pulse in the antecubital fossa. This is where you'll place your stethoscope. Take a moment to feel the pulse clearly.

👆

Click to palpate pulse

4

Inflate and Listen

Place stethoscope earpieces in your ears and the diaphragm over the brachial artery. Close the valve on the bulb and inflate the cuff rapidly to about 180 mmHg (30 mmHg above expected systolic). Then slowly release air at 2-3 mmHg per second while listening.

🩺

Click to take measurement

5

Record Results

The first sound you heard (Korotkoff sound) is the systolic pressure. When the sounds disappear completely, that's the diastolic pressure. Record both values and the arm used.

Classification: Normal

Athlete Considerations

Athletes often have lower resting heart rates and blood pressure due to cardiovascular conditioning. Ensure the patient hasn't exercised recently. Note that athletic patients may have readings of 110/70 or even lower, which is normal for them.

Athletic heart: Lower BP and heart rate are expected. Readings around 105-110/65-70 mmHg are typical.
2

Standard Procedure

Follow the same measurement technique: position cuff, locate pulse, inflate to 180 mmHg, and release slowly at 2-3 mmHg per second. Listen carefully for the first and last Korotkoff sounds.

Review the measurement and compare with typical athletic norms. Document the results and note this is within normal range for a conditioned athlete.

1

Elderly Patient Considerations

Older patients may have stiffer arteries, making accurate measurement more challenging. Ensure proper rest time (5+ minutes), check for orthostatic hypotension if needed, and be gentle with fragile skin when applying the cuff.

Age-related changes: Arteries become less elastic. Isolated systolic hypertension is common. Target BP for this patient should be <130/80 mmHg.
2

Careful Technique

Position the cuff gently, ensuring it's at heart level. Elderly patients may need a larger cuff size. Inflate slowly to avoid discomfort, and listen carefully as sounds may be fainter.

🩺

Click to take measurement

3

Evaluate and Document

Compare the reading to the patient's target range and previous measurements. For patients on antihypertensive medication, consistent monitoring is crucial.

Classification: Well-controlled on medication
Within target range (<130/80 mmHg)
Previous reading (2 weeks ago): 128/80 mmHg

Heart Rate Assessment

Learn proper pulse palpation technique and accurate heart rate measurement

Patient: David Chen

Age
45 years
Gender

State

Site
Radial artery
1

Position Patient and Locate Radial Artery

Have the patient sit or lie comfortably with arm supported and wrist relaxed. Using your index and middle fingers (never use your thumb as it has its own pulse), locate the radial artery on the thumb side of the wrist, just below the base of the thumb.

✋

Place two fingers on radial artery

Important: Use your INDEX and MIDDLE fingers only. Your thumb has its own pulse and will give false readings.
2

Feel the Pulse Quality

Before counting, assess the pulse quality. Is it strong or weak? Regular or irregular? Note the rhythm. Apply gentle pressure—too much will obliterate the pulse, too little and you won't feel it clearly.

💓

Click to palpate pulse rhythm

3

Count the Pulse

Using a watch with a second hand or timer, count the beats for 30 seconds and multiply by 2. For irregular pulses, count for a full 60 seconds. Start counting on a beat as "zero" then count from there.

⏱️

Click to start 30-second count

4

Record and Interpret

Calculate the heart rate (beats counted × 2 if 30-second count). Normal resting heart rate for adults is 60-100 bpm. Record the rate, rhythm, and site.

Rhythm: Regular
Quality: Strong, easily palpable
Site: Right radial artery
Classification: Normal sinus rhythm

1

When to Use Carotid Pulse

The carotid pulse is used when peripheral pulses are difficult to palpate (shock, hypotension), during CPR assessment, or in emergency situations. It's stronger and more reliable than peripheral pulses in critical conditions.

CAUTION: Never palpate both carotid arteries simultaneously—this can reduce blood flow to the brain. Use gentle pressure. Avoid in patients with carotid artery disease.
2

Locate Carotid Artery

Position yourself beside the patient. Use your index and middle fingers to locate the larynx (Adam's apple). Slide your fingers laterally into the groove between the trachea and the sternocleidomastoid muscle. You should feel the pulse within 1-2 cm of the trachea.

👤

Lateral to trachea, medial to SCM muscle

3

Assess and Count

Apply gentle pressure to feel the pulse. Count for 30-60 seconds depending on urgency and regularity. In emergencies, even a 6-second count (×10) can give a rapid assessment.

⏱️

Click to count carotid pulse

4

Document Findings

Record the pulse rate, quality, and rhythm. Note that carotid pulse was used instead of radial and document the reason (e.g., "radial pulse weak due to hypotension").

Heart Rate: 88 bpm
Site: Left carotid artery
Quality: Strong, bounding
Rhythm: Regular
Note: Radial pulse weak but carotid pulse easily palpable

1

Post-Exercise Guidelines

After exercise, heart rate will be elevated. Normal response is 140-180+ bpm immediately after vigorous activity. Take the pulse immediately (within 5 seconds of stopping) for the most accurate peak reading, then reassess during recovery.

Recovery heart rate: HR should drop by at least 12 bpm in the first minute after exercise. A slower recovery may indicate poor cardiovascular fitness or other issues.
2

Immediate Post-Exercise Count

Quickly locate the radial or carotid pulse. For speed, count for 6 seconds and multiply by 10, or count for 10 seconds and multiply by 6. This gives a rapid assessment of peak heart rate.

🏃⏱️

Click for 10-second count

3

Monitor Recovery

Recheck the pulse after 1 minute of rest. Compare to the immediate post-exercise reading. A healthy recovery shows a significant drop (>12 bpm is good, >20 bpm is excellent).

⏳

Click to check 1-minute recovery

4

Interpret and Record

Document peak HR, recovery HR, and the change. Assess cardiovascular response to exercise. Normal recovery indicates good cardiovascular fitness.

Exercise Heart Rate Assessment:
Peak HR (immediate): 168 bpm
1-min Recovery HR: 142 bpm
HR Drop: 26 bpm (Excellent recovery)
5-min Recovery HR: 88 bpm
Assessment: Normal cardiovascular response to exercise

Reflex Testing

Master neurological reflex assessment using proper hammer technique

Patient: Michael Anderson

Age
35 years
Gender
Male
Test
Patellar (knee-jerk) reflex
Reason
Routine neurological exam
1

Position the Patient

Have the patient sit on the edge of the exam table with legs dangling freely, not touching the floor. This relaxes the quadriceps muscle. Alternatively, if the patient is lying down, support the knee in a slightly flexed position (about 90 degrees).

🦵

Legs hanging freely, quadriceps relaxed

Key: The muscle must be completely relaxed. Distraction techniques (having patient interlock fingers and pull apart) can help anxious patients relax.
2

Locate the Patellar Tendon

Place your thumb on the patient's patella (kneecap). The patellar tendon is the cord-like structure just below the patella that connects to the tibial tuberosity. This is your target—about 1-2 inches below the bottom of the kneecap.

👆🦵

Patellar tendon: just below kneecap

3

Strike with Reflex Hammer

Hold the reflex hammer loosely at the end of the handle. Use a quick, snapping wrist motion (not an arm swing) to strike the patellar tendon directly. The strike should be firm but not hard— you're testing a neurological pathway, not strength. Strike perpendicular to the tendon.

🔨

Click to perform reflex test

4

Observe and Grade Response

Watch for the quadriceps muscle to contract, causing the lower leg to kick forward (extend). Grade the reflex on a 0-4 scale:
0 = Absent
1+ = Diminished/low normal
2+ = Normal
3+ = Brisk, more than normal
4+ = Hyperactive with clonus (rhythmic contractions)

Patellar Reflex Results:
Right knee: 2+ (Normal)
Left knee: 2+ (Normal)
Response: Symmetrical, brisk extension of both lower legs
Interpretation: Normal deep tendon reflex, intact L2-L4 nerve roots

1

Position for Achilles Test

Have the patient kneel on a chair with feet hanging over the edge, or have them lie prone with feet extending off the table. Alternatively, if sitting, dorsiflex the foot slightly by holding the ball of the foot and gently pushing it toward the shin to put tension on the Achilles tendon.

🦶

Ankle in dorsiflexion, foot relaxed

2

Locate Achilles Tendon

The Achilles tendon is the thick cord at the back of the ankle connecting the calf muscles to the heel. Place your hand on the ball of the foot to stabilize and slightly dorsiflex. The target area is about 2-3 inches above where the tendon inserts into the heel bone.

Clinical significance: Achilles reflex tests S1-S2 nerve roots. Diminished or absent response may indicate nerve root compression from disc herniation.
3

Perform Test

Strike the Achilles tendon with the reflex hammer using the same quick, snapping wrist motion. The expected response is plantar flexion—the foot pointing downward as the calf muscle contracts. Test both sides and compare.

🔨

Click to test Achilles reflex

4

Grade and Document

Grade the response using the same 0-4 scale. Document side-to-side differences, as asymmetry can be clinically significant. In this patient with lower back pain, diminished reflexes could indicate nerve root involvement.

Achilles Reflex Results:
Right ankle: 2+ (Normal plantar flexion)
Left ankle: 1+ (Diminished response)

⚠️ Clinical Note: Asymmetric reflexes with diminished left Achilles reflex. Combined with patient's left foot numbness and lower back pain, this suggests possible S1 nerve root compression. Recommend further evaluation.

1

Understanding Abnormal Reflexes

Abnormal reflexes can indicate various conditions:

Absent/Diminished (0-1+): Lower motor neuron lesions, peripheral neuropathy (common in diabetes), nerve root compression, muscle disorders

Hyperactive (3-4+): Upper motor neuron lesions, spinal cord injury above the reflex level, stroke, multiple sclerosis, hyperthyroidism

2

Test Multiple Reflexes

In this patient with diabetes and neuropathy, test both upper extremity (biceps, triceps) and lower extremity (patellar, Achilles) reflexes. This creates a complete neurological picture and helps identify the pattern of involvement.

🔨📋

Click to perform complete reflex exam

3

Interpret Pattern

Look at the overall pattern of findings. In peripheral neuropathy, you typically see diminished or absent reflexes in a "stocking-glove" distribution—affecting distal extremities (feet and hands) more than proximal areas. Lower extremity reflexes are often more affected than upper.

Complete Reflex Examination Results:

Upper Extremities:
Biceps: 2+/2+ (Normal bilaterally)
Triceps: 2+/2+ (Normal bilaterally)

Lower Extremities:
Patellar: 1+/1+ (Diminished bilaterally)
Achilles: 0/0 (Absent bilaterally)

Pattern: Distal lower extremity reflexes more affected than proximal and upper extremities.
Interpretation: Consistent with length-dependent peripheral neuropathy, typical of diabetic neuropathy. The longest nerves (to feet) are affected first and most severely.
4

Clinical Correlation

Always correlate reflex findings with the patient's history, symptoms, and other physical exam findings. In this case, absent ankle reflexes with a history of diabetes supports the diagnosis of diabetic peripheral neuropathy. Document clearly and consider referral if indicated.

Documentation complete. Findings consistent with patient's known condition. Recommend continued diabetes management and regular neurological monitoring.
